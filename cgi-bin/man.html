Kincaid Formula

The grade-level Kincaid Formula was developed for Navy training manuals that ranged in difficulty from 5.5 to 16.3. It is probably best applied to technical documents, because it is based on adult training manuals rather than school book text. Passages of dialog, which are usually a series of short sentences, lower the score. On the other hand, scientific texts with many long scientific terms are rated higher, although they are not neccessarily harder to read for people who are familar with the terms. 

    Kincaid = 11.8*syllables/words+0.39*words/sentences-15.59

Automated Readability Index

The Automated Readability Index is typically higher than Kincaid and Coleman-Liau, but lower than Flesch. 

    ARI = 4.71*chars/words+0.5*words/sentences-21.43

Coleman-Liau Formula

The Coleman-Liau Formula usually gives a lower grade than Kincaid, ARI and Flesch when applied to technical docu- ments. 

    Coleman-Liau = 5.89*chars/words-0.3*sentences/(100*words)-15.8

Flesh Reading Ease Formula

The Flesh Reading Ease formula, which is not a grade-level formula, instead rates the readability of a passage on a scale from 0 (very hard) to 100 (very easy). That is, the *higher* a passage scores, the *easier* it should be to read. Standard English documents average approximately 60 to 70. 

    Flesch Index = 206.835-84.6*syll/words-1.015*words/sentences

Fog Index

The Fog index has been developed by Robert Gunning. Its value is a school grade. The ``ideal'' Fog Index level is 7 or 8. A level above 12 indicates the writing sample is too hard for most people to read. Only use it on texts of at least hundred words to get meaningful results. Note that a correct implementation would not count words of three or more syllables that are proper names, combina- tions of easy words, or made three syllables by suffixes such as -ed, -es, or -ing. 

    Fog Index = 0.4*(words/sentences+100*((words >= 3 syll)/words))

WSTF Index

The first new Vienna text formula (1. neue Wiener Sach- textformel, WSTF) has been developed for German documents and its result is a school grade that could read the text. 

    WSTF Index =  0.1935*(words >= 3 syllables)/words
               +0.1672*words/sentences
               -0.1297*(words > 6 characters)/words
               -0.0327*(words = 1 syllable)/words
               -0.875

Wheeler-Smith Index

The Wheeler-Smith Index is mapped to school grades using a table: 

    Wheeler-Smith Index = words/sentences*100*((words >= 3 syll)/words)

    Index         16       20       24       29       34       38       42
    School year    5        6        7        8        9       10

Lix formula

The Lix formula developed by Bjrnsson from Sweden is very simple and employs a mapping table as well: 

    Lix = words/sentences+100*(words >= 6 char)/words

    Index         34       38       41       44       48       51        54       57
    School year    5        6        7        8        9       10        11

SMOG-Grading

The SMOG-Grading for English texts has been developed by McLaughlin in 1969. Its result is a school grade. 

    SMOG-Grading = square root of ((words >= 3 syll)*sentences*30) + 3
It has been adapted to German by Bamberger & Vanecek in 1984, who changed the constant +3 to -2.
