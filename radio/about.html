About the specially modified shortwave radio english broadcast preparation engine

Output text produced by the specially modified shortwave radio English broadcast preparation engine is essentially a simulation of a broadcast instance of the specially modified shortwave radio English dialect. The output text is generated using a complex algorithm that attempts to precisely follow certain linguistic rules common to observed instances of specially modified shortwave radio English.

The algorithm

Note that because no prescriptive grammar exists for the dialect, the algorithm is limited in that it represents a "reverse engineering" or reproduction of some, but far from all, of the linguistic rules of the dialect. However, the algorithm is based on a rigorous linguistic analysis of a very large sampling of recorded broadcast instances, and will for the most part generate text that is valid according to the observed rules, though certainly less complex than most actual instances.

That is, an average actual broadcast recording of specially modified shortwave English is likely to contain a number of instances of rules that the algorithm at this point can't reproduce. (Some of the more obscure broadcast instances are in fact so complex that most of their underlying rules are not yet completely understood.) Overall, though, the specially modified shortwave radio English broadcast preparation engine development team believes that most users will be very satisfied with the results the algorithm generates.

Loan words

One noticeable feature of specially modified shortwave radio English is its occasional use of "loan words" from non-English languages, including (sometimes) the use of words that are themselves concatenations or combinations of terms from multiple languages. The algorithm attempts to reproduce this feature of the dialect. However, the development team currently views this as the most problematic part of the algorithm and wants to warn users that it can sometimes produce unpredictable transient results -- including, on occasion, generated text that is predominantly in another language. These invalid transient results are fairly easy to spot, however, and you generally need only reload the results page to eliminate the transient results and produce valid text instead.

Why "seed text"?

The development team initially created the component of the engine that generates random ready-for-broadcast text. But some users who participated in an early field test of the engine found the random text vaguely unsatisfying, as did some members of the development team. Then it was suggested that the team could enhance the engine by adding a component that manipulates a passage of standard English "seed text" to produce ready-for-broadcast text that "looks like" or seems to "mean" something similar to the standard English input. The team quickly realized that developing such an engine would be quite possible and that the output it generated might in some ways be more interesting than the random text. So, after several months of additional development, the "seed text" component was added and the Web interface to the engine was made publicly available, basically in the same form in which you see it now.

Note that use of seed text does not turn the specially modified shortwave radio English broadcast preparation engine into a "translation engine". Its underlying algorithm can in fact be altered in such a way that, based on the same seed text, it would produce a very different instance of the dialect than the instance it currently produces for that seed text. It's even possible that two different passages of standard English used as seed text could each produce identical instances of specially modified shortwave radio English (though this would be very difficult to actually demonstrate). Thus, it makes little sense to speak of "translation" between specially modified shortwave radio English and standard English (or between the dialect and any other language, for that matter).

In fact, by its very nature, specially modified shortwave radio English is fundamentally untranslatable. The rules of the dialect do not require that individual words have any absolute meaning -- only a "sense" relative to the other words within the instances in which they occur. Taken as a whole, an instance of the dialect can't really be said to "mean" anything, because unlike most other languages, the dialect does not have as a purpose the idea of "conveying meaning".

Future development

The team continues it analysis of the dialect and will eventually make its research results available to the language community for review. However, the team also feels strongly that there is a value to making the initial results of their research -- in the form of the current specially modified shortwave radio English broadcast preparation engine -- available to the general public. The team will of course continue to refine the engine as their knowledge of the dialect increases. They welcome your comments and suggestions and can be contacted at:
